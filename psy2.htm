The Brain and Behavior: A Brief Historical Introduction

 The brain has been generally acknowledged as the organ of behavior and the seat of consciousness.  Our knowledge, skills and abilities lie within the brain, and who we are is defined for ourselves and the rest of the world by the activity of our brains.  The idea that the brain is the seat of the personality and the determinant of behavior is fairly new in human history.  Plato believed that the mind was located in the head; his student Aristotle, however, believed it was in the heart.  So did the ancient Egyptians, who preserved the heart and other organs of the deceased during the 60 day mummification process. The Egyptians thought the brain was involved only in regulating nasal secretions, and hence was given little significance to the quality of the afterlife.  The internal organs of the deceased were often embalmed and placed into separate jars to accompany the body at the burial site. The cold, gray, silent brain was simply removed through the nose and tossed away.
 

Aristotle's idea was dominant until the 1800's, when the German physiologist Franz Gall proposed that various abilities and personality characteristics were localized in the brain, and that the dominance of each of these personality traits was expressed by the size of the lump on the skull over the brain area in question.  Gall's theory and practice of phrenology enjoyed a brief period of popularity in Britain and North America, with traveling phrenologists giving skull readings.  But a man injured in a railroad accident became the subject of a case study which first demonstrated the relationship between brain injury and  behavior.

Our story begins on September 13, 1848, when a railroad foreman in Vermont named Phineas Gage was hammering blasting powder into a hole with a three-and-a-half foot tamping iron. This was standard operating procedure: the powder was simply poured into a hole drilled six feet into the rock face.  The powder was compressed in the hole by blows from the tamping iron. Guess what happened.  The premature blast sent the tamping iron into his left cheek, behind his left eye and out the top of his head.  He walked away from the accident, was taken to a nearby town and treated by a doctor, a former railroad man himself, who wrote that he could touch the fingers of both his hands inside Gage's head as he cleaned out bits of bone and dirt.  Gage survived the bout of infection following his injury and looked as though he was completely recovered in a couple of weeks.
 

By then, the long term effects of his injury were becoming apparent to the people around him.  Before his injury, Gage was a steady and dependable railroad employee, and a polite and sociable gentleman.  Following the accident, Gage became abusive, foul mouthed and antisocial.  His contemporaries said he related better to animals than to people. He remained unbalanced, and lived another 12 years, working odd jobs and exhibiting himself as a sideshow curiosity.
 

Gage's unfortunate accident was the first clinical case which documented the relationship between behavior, personality, and damage to the brain. In Gage's day, scientists thought that behavior was a product of the heart or the gut. We now know that he probably severed the connections between the "thinking" part of his brain (the frontal cortex) and the "feeling" part of his brain (the limbic system) in the mid brain). Gage's judgment, emotions and social inhibitions were severely affected by the accident. Normal brain function is largely a product of nerve cell inhibition-  too much excitation in neural networks can be bad news for normal brain functioning: seizure disorders, for example, are caused by over-excitation of neural networks in the brain.
 

 Head injuries put a half million Americans in the hospital each year. The relationship between head injuries and subsequent changes in behavior  covers a wide variety of outcomes: some people recover with no apparent aftereffects, while others, sustaining similar damage, are seriously disabled in their social and intellectual functioning.  Rehabilitation is often a long and arduous process, with  patients often exhibiting  deficits in cognitive, emotional-  and perceptual processing, physical disabilities (such as paralysis), and significant personality changes. About 70% of these brain injuries result in personality changes  including  increased irritability, frustration, and aggressiveness.  Approximately   two-thirds of the patients seen at the soon-to-be-moved  US Army's  Walter Reed Medical Center have head injuries sustained  in the Iraq and Afghanistan wars.  The "signature wound" of the Iraq war is traumatic brain injury, with about 22  percent of the wounded soldiers, Marines and sailors sustaining disabling brain injuries from roadside bombs, vehicle accidents or gunfire.    Due to the swiftness of medical treatment on the battlefield, many people survive wounds that would have been fatal in the Vietnam War or in the Gulf War of 1990.  How some of these grievously injured people will able to live and function in society is an open question. As of this writing, the US Veterans' Administration has restored some funding supporting these injured soldiers, many of whom will need a lifetime of skilled nursing care. America's has a long history (about 200 years) of neglecting its veterans, with the exception of mostly white veterans from WWII and Korea, who were provided a reasonably decent benefit package for their service.

Humankind's knowledge of the brain and its relationship to behavior and genetics has given us tools and investigative techniques that can track the activity of small parts of the brain as the organ is put through its paces in learning, language and memory tasks.  Advances in brain chemistry have enabled us to understand the biology of addictions, obsessive compulsive behavior, schizophrenia and depression. Toxicologists have discovered the effects on the brain of a host of human-made chemicals in the environment, and medical researchers are on the trail of such degenerative diseases as Alzheimer's, multiple sclerosis and stroke.  But before we go into detail about the structure and function of brain systems, we will take a look at the basic unit of the nervous system, the nerve cell, or neuron.
 

Neurons: The Basic Unit of the Nervous System


The nerve cell, or neuron, is the fundamental decision unit in the vast information-processing system that comprises the brain and central nervous system. The neuron, like a transistor or a light switch, has two functioning states: on and off.  When the neuron is off, it is not sending an electrochemical impulse to the next ("downstream") cell.  When the neuron is on, it is sending an electrochemical impulse to all the "downstream" neurons connected with it. The nerve impulse typically takes about a thousandth of a second, and its strength (or amplitude) is the same time after time.  The intensity of the "message" the neuron sends to the "downstream" cells is defined by the number of times per second the neuron fires.  The neuron works on the "all or none" principle: when the amount of stimulation reaches a fixed threshold, the neuron fires an impulse. The greater the stimulation of the nerve cell, the faster it fires.

The neuron is divided into three basic parts: the dendrites (which look like the branches of a tree under the microscope),  which receive impulses from "upstream" neurons; the cell body, and the axon- which is the "cable" over which the neuron sends its outgoing impulses to the downstream neurons. Neurons can connect to other neurons, muscles or glands.  The axon is typically covered with a fatty sheath (called myelin) which serves to insulate it from the rest of the world, much like a telephone wire is covered with rubber or plastic to keep it from "shorting out."  Multiple sclerosis is a disease which attacks the myelin insulation, causing neurons to muscles to "short out," resulting in the loss of muscle control. Myelin not only insulates axons from one another, it greatly speeds transmission of nerve impulses because entire sections of axon are activated at once, rather than the impulse gradually progressing from cell body to the tip of the axon.  Myelinated axons have a top transmission speed of about 200 miles per hour, which is much slower than the speed of electricity down a wire.

A neuron fires an impulse when it is stimulated by an adjacent neuron, or by a sensory receptor which converts one form of energy (light, as in the case of the light receptors at the back of the eye) into the electrochemical energy currency of the neuron.  All of our sensory receptors are "transducers:" they convert temperature, pressure, sound, light, or chemical energy into electrochemical energy.  The image of a nerve impulse sweeping down the axon is similar to that of a row of dominoes falling.  If you push the first domino with enough force, the entire row goes down, one domino at a time.

The nerve impulse is referred to as the "action potential:" a term borrowed from electrical engineering to describe a wave of electrical activity traveling down the axon.  The axon is basically a semi-permeable membrane with holes that are just small enough to keep out positively charged sodium ions, but large enough to let the negatively charged chloride ions pass through.  When you dissolve table salt in water, you get a mixture of sodium and chloride ions floating around in the water.  Salt is necessary for animal life because its components are key to nerve functioning. When the nerve impulse begins, the holes in the axon membrane enlarge, permitting the entry of the positively charged sodium ions into the axon.  Almost instantly, the inside of the axon reverses its charge (or polarity), from negative to positive. An instant later (under a thousandth of a second), the large, positively charged sodium ions are pumped out by a mechanism called- you guessed it, the "sodium pump." A nerve impulse amounts to a traveling wave of depolarization (reversal of charge from negative to positive, and back to negative again) moving along the membrane of the axon.

Neurons receive messages from other neurons at their dendrites or their cell bodies.  Incoming messages can be of two kinds: excitatory and inhibitory, depending on the properties of the connections (the synapses) between the targeted neuron and the neurons upstream.  A neuron acts as a decision unit, tallying up the "yes votes" (the excitatory messages) and the "no votes" (the inhibitory messages) from the upstream neurons. The neuron fires if the excitatory messages outnumber the inhibitory messages.  But that's not all.  Synapses are not created equal in terms of their "voting rights: those closer to the cell body are closer to the origin of the axon and its impulse generator (called the axon hillock).  Synapses terminating close to the axon hillock get the most priority. For synapses to have influence on triggering the cell they are adjacent to, it's a matter of location, location, location.
 

Where the Action Is: The Synapse


For years,  neurophysiologists thought that neurons were physically connected with one another, until the British physiologist Charles Sherrington discovered a tiny gap between neurons, which he  called the synaptic gap or cleft. One function of the synaptic gap is to ensure that nerve impulses flow in one direction only: downstream.  The synaptic gap serves as a one way valve in the neural circuit, much like the purpose served by a diode in electronic circuits.  When the wave of depolarization reaches the synaptic cleft, it causes the release of tiny packets of neurotransmitter, which migrate across the synaptic gap and embed themselves in receptors much like a key fits in a lock.  If the "key" fits the "lock," the downstream (or post synaptic) cell admits the packet of neurotransmitter inside of the cell, causing its membrane to open its holes to the large sodium ions in the surrounding fluid, causing the cell membrane to depolarize.  If enough synapses  send neurotransmitters to the post synaptic cell, the post synaptic cell will fire.  However, please keep in mind that some synapses have an excitatory effect (they tend to depolarize the post synaptic cell) while other synapses are inhibitory (they tend to polarize the post synaptic cell even more than it is in its resting state).

The essence of communication between neurons lies in the various kinds of neurotransmitters: so far, about 75 neurotransmitters have been discovered.  Some neurotransmitters play a crucial role in heart function, others can alter mood, and still others can influence alertness.  One of the earliest neurotransmitters to give up its secrets is acetylcholine (ACh).  ACh mediates the transmission of nerve impulses between neurons and muscles at a structure called the motor end plate, which is the muscle's "connection" to the nerve cells upstream.  The muscle contracts When ACh is released from the neuron across the motor end plate.  If the transmission of ACh is blocked, the muscle cannot contract.  For thousands of years, some South- and Central American Indians have refined  a potent toxin called curare from the bark of certain trees, and used it to coat the tips of their hunting darts.  Curare occupies the ACh receptor sites, rendering ACh ineffective in transmitting nerve impulses.  Animals struck by these poison darts become paralyzed.  Modern medicine also uses curare to paralyze muscles in patients undergoing surgery, permitting the surgeon to work without the hazards of muscles twitching at inopportune times.  A variant of curare is also used in electroconvulsive therapy for chronically depressed patients to keep them from thrashing around during their electrically induced seizures.  The sometimes fatal disease called botulism is caused by a toxin that is produced by bacteria in improperly canned food.  The toxin, botulin, causes paralysis by blocking the release of ACh from the sending neuron.  Botulin, by the way, is the active ingredient in the popular wrinkle-removing drug Botox. The venom of the black widow spider, on the other hand, causes a flood of ACh in the synapses, causing violent muscle contractions, convulsions, and possibly death.  The bacteria which cause tetanus release a toxin having a similar effect on the synapses as the venom of the black widow spider. Many pesticides (such as the so-called organophosphates) kill insects by acting at the synapses to interfere with the action of neurotransmitters. More recently, chemicals found in the venom of Australia's cone snail  have been found to have pain killing abilities that rival morphine, and without morphine's sedative effect.  Many pesticides (such as Malathion) are basically "nerve gas for bugs:" chemical warfare agents and pesticides are produced in much the same way, and have much the same effects on their victims.

Back in 1973 a class of neurotransmitters known as endorphins was discovered.  Endorphin literally means "the morphine within." This research grew out of efforts to find the receptor sites in the brain for morphine; as it turned out, the receptor sites were in areas of the brain which deal with mood and the sensation of pain. Several types of endorphins have been found, and each has a molecule shaped similar to the morphine molecule. One thing that biologists have learned is that nature never does anything for fun, the endorphins probably serve the function of lessening pain and suffering in animals.  Indeed, endorphins are released in response to pain and during vigorous physical activity.  Endorphins may be the basis of the "runner's high." (Bradley-Popovich, 2007) Recent evidence suggests that neurotransmitters are intimately involved in the process of falling in love.

Studies of addiction have shown that when people abuse opiates over a long period of time, they produce less endorphins, so when they stop taking the opiates, they experience  withdrawal symptoms in the form of body aches and pains, nausea, uncontrollable shaking and kicking (hence the expression "kicking the habit").  This persists until their bodies start making more endorphins, or until they get more opiates.

Researchers have created a wide variety of drugs that work at the synaptic junction by binding with receptor sites for the body's natural neurotransmitters. Agonists are a class of drug which mimics the shape and chemical action of a particular neurotransmitter molecule, simulating its pharmacological effects. Morphine, codeine, and synthetic narcotics are examples of agonists: they achieve pain relief and mood elevation by binding to the receptor sites which are designed to accommodate endorphins.

Antagonists work by blocking the action of natural neurotransmitters at their receptor sites. Curare is an example, as are certain neurotoxins found in venomous snakes which block the transmission of nerve impulses, causing paralysis and death. Some of the deadliest snakes in the world are found in Australia, and many are sea snakes, whose neurotoxic venom can take you out of the gene pool in fairly short order.  The blue ringed octopus is another deadly denizen of the seas off Australia, possessed of a potent neurotoxin that can kill a person in minutes.  There is no antidote to this poison. No bigger than a golf ball, this little guy packs enough poison to kill 26 adults.

Other drugs work to slow down the re-uptake or absorption of natural neurotransmitters at the synapse, allowing the natural neurotransmitters to have their effects for a longer period of time.  The antidepressant Prozac works like this: it allows the neurotransmitter serotonin to work at the synapse for a longer period of time than would otherwise be possible. Prozac is also useful in the treatment of panic disorder, and has been a subject of widespread social commentary about the role of drugs in American society(Cushman, 1993).
 

Systems of Neurons


Neurons form the basis of the central nervous system, and play a major role in the body's endocrine system, which executes a very subtle and discreet form of communication between organ systems.  The brain and spinal cord comprise the central nervous system (CNS). The peripheral nervous system (PNS) integrates the output of the body's sense receptors, muscles and glands.  A nerve is a bundle of many axons or dendrites, often originating at a sensory receptor (such as the eye) and leading to areas in the brain or spinal cord.  A nerve is analogous to a telephone cable, containing many individual fibers. The optic nerve, which begins at the back of the eye and projects to the brain, contains nearly a million axon fibers.  The eyes are actually considered part of the central nervous system, since they are derived from brain tissue during embryonic development.

Different types of neurons comprise the nervous system.  Sensory neurons send information from the sense organs to the brain. Sensory neurons providing data on sight, hearing, taste and smell project directly to the brain.  Sensory neurons providing data on skin temperature, pressure, pain and muscle tension project to the spinal cord, where they are processed before being sent to the brain.  Sensory data from all the organs and blood vessels of the body are routed through the spinal cord, where many reflexes which mediate locomotor behavior and withdrawal from painful stimuli originate. Recent findings of neurons which fire when an animal either performs an action or sees another animal perform it have excited neuroscientists.  These "mirror neurons" are found in some birds and primates.  In humans these neurons have been found in the areas of the brain that process speech and language. They may play a significant role in our ability to imitate others' behavior and in our ability to learn from observing the consequences of another's behavior.

The brain and spinal cord are replete with interneurons, which mediate communication between other neurons and are arranged into elaborate networks. Much of the complexity of the brain takes the form of vast networks of interneurons which have no direct contact with the outside world.  Their job is to integrate and process information from other neurons. Many of these neural networks are constantly changing their circuit architecture in response to learning and memory events.  Most of the central nervous system is made up of billions and billions of  interneurons, and only about ten million sensory and motor neurons.

Disturbances in these neural networks can alter a person's grasp of reality. For instance,  the abuse of amphetamines can lead to an  amphetamine psychosis, the mechanism of which can be traced to  the increase of neurotransmitters called catecholamines  being released into  the synaptic cleft by interneurons. Amphetamines also destroy connections between neurons in the brain's pleasure and reward centers, making it difficult for the drug user to experience pleasure without the aid of the drug.  It can take as long as a year of sobriety for a former amphetamine user to begin feeling pleasure again as these nerve connections grow back.  In the 1980's, Oregon was the methamphetamine capital of the United States, the center of the manufacture and distribution of this cheap, highly addicting drug(Doyle, 2006).  Outlaw biker gangs such as the Pagans, the Gypsy Jokers, the Hell's Angels and the Brothers Speed controlled the  supply and distribution of methamphetamine.  Since then, Mexican cartels, with sophisticated, high output "superlabs" have taken over the meth market, and cities all across the United States are feeling the impact of the methamphetamine epidemic, expressed in escalating property crimes and ID thefts,  including crimes of violence.  The drug is easy to make: people with no chemistry background can learn how to "cook" meth using recipes available over the Internet. Methamphetamine is rapidly becoming the drug of choice in Eastern European countries as well as in Russia.

The peripheral nervous system consists  of the skeletal nervous system, which exercises voluntary control over our muscles, and the autonomic nervous system, which controls the glands and the muscles of our internal organs (the heart and the intestines, for example).  As the name "autonomic" implies, this system functions automatically, with little conscious thought given to its activity.  We seldom "take ownership" of our liver or intestines- this is handled on a day to day basis by control centers in the brain stem.

The autonomic nervous system does play a role in emotional arousal, however.  When we are emotionally aroused, the autonomic nervous system co ordinates the complex symphony of hormonal, muscle and organ systems to enable us to fight to defend ourselves or run away from danger.  This subsystem, called the  sympathetic nervous system evolved during the age of dinosaurs to give its owners an edge in the struggle for survival.  When activated, the sympathetic nervous system increases heart rate, respiration and blood pressure, and releases adrenaline and other hormones into the bloodstream to prepare the organism for action.  When the stress subsides, the parasympathetic nervous system takes over to lower arousal and bring the body back to a normal state of equilibrium.  Both the sympathetic and the parasympathetic nervous systems work hand in hand to maintain a steady internal state.  And let's not forget the immense enteric nervous system which runs the digestive system and has as many neurons as the spinal cord.  This system is the "Rodney Dangerfield" of the body's nervous systems: it gets no respect.  Somehow, studying the intestines is not as "sexy" for neuroscientists as studying the brain.
 

The body's main nerve cable is the spinal cord, which contains sensory and motor neurons, as well as a host of interneurons that mediate a variety of reflexes, from withdrawing one's finger from a painful stimulus before the sensation of pain registers in the brain, to reflexes needed for walking and moving around in a coordinated manner.   Damage to the spinal cord through trauma or disease can result in loss of function in sensory and motor neurons located below the site of damage.  The result of such damage is the inability to feel anything below the damaged site, or voluntarily move muscles controlled by neurons below the damaged site.
 

The Endocrine System


The endocrine system is the body's chemical communication system, which works in concert with the central nervous system.  Neurons in the brain send electrochemical impulses to glands such as the pituitary (located just above the roof of the mouth) and adrenal glands (located just above each kidney). These glands secrete chemical messengers called hormones directly into the bloodstream, where they migrate to a variety of target organs, including the brain. Hormones targeting the brain influence our interest in food, sex and aggression.  Indeed, some of these hormones are identical to neurotransmitters (adrenaline is one), the difference in effect is dependent on the method of delivery:  electrochemical nerve impulses travel through the body at relatively high speed, chemical messengers take considerably longer.  But their effects on targeted organs persist longer.  An example is the adrenaline mediated emotional arousal response: the effects of adrenaline persist for as long as 20 minutes; the effects of electrochemical nerve impulses dissipate after only a few seconds.

The body's "master gland" is the pituitary gland, which is about the size of a pea and is connected directly to the brain by two sets of fibers: one carrying nerve impulses, the other carrying chemicals made in the brain  (by neurosecretory cells) and piped down to the pituitary gland. The pituitary gland regulates growth, and until the advent of synthetic human growth hormone, children destined for abnormally short stature were treated with doses of human growth hormone extracted from about 300 cadaver pituitaries.  Recently, allegations have been made that human growth hormone has been used by some parents to enhance the growth (and thereby the athletic potential) of their children(Randerson, 2004).  A child who might ordinarily  grow to six foot five inches and weigh 250 pounds could, with the help of synthetic human growth hormone could  grow to six foot ten inches and weigh 325 pounds.

Ordinarily, the brain and endocrine systems form a very  effective and discrete feedback loop, where the brain influences the pituitary gland to secrete hormones affecting other glands in the body (such as the ovaries and testes), that in turn secrete hormones (such as testosterone and estrogen)  which then affect the brain, and consequently, behavior.  In some cases (adrenaline, for example), the differences between neurotransmitters and hormones are not clear at all.  But the organ which orchestrates this electrochemical and hormonal symphony is the brain.
 

The Brain


The brain is the seat of consciousness and awareness, the brain is the house of the mind.  For the human mind to exist requires a brain. Evidence dating back to the stone age indicates that people have survived having holes drilled in their skulls (presumably to reduce head pain, or perhaps to let evil spirits out) long enough for new bone to have grown back. The brain workings  have  mystified humanity since then,  but the brain was not systematically studied until the nineteenth century. In 1865 the French physician Paul Broca worked with a patient he called "Tan" who could understand- but not produce speech.  The only word he could say was "tan" although he was able to sing familiar songs and understand what you were saying to him.  When Tan died, Broca removed his brain and discovered massive damage to the left parietal lobe.  Broca drew the conclusion that speech was processed in this area and that mental function was localized to discrete parts of the brain (Cowie, 2000).  In 1870 The German physicians Gustav Fritsch and Eduard Hitzig delivered mild electric currents to the brains of dogs and found that they could make different body parts move when they stimulated different parts of the dogs' brains.  In the 1940's the neurosurgeon Wilder Penfield used an electric current to map the brains of conscious patients undergoing brain surgery.  He wanted to map the surface of the brain in order to avoid cutting into areas which were crucial for speech, memory and motor function.  Penfield found that by stimulating different parts of the brain, he could evoke memories, sensations, or movements in patients. Essentials of his technique are standard surgical operating procedure to this day. 
 
 

Brain Imaging Techniques

Today's brain researchers have an impressive array of tools and techniques to study the brain. The electrical activity in the brain's billions of neurons can be studied using the electroencephalograph (EEG).  The EEG employs a number of electrodes which are pasted to a person's scalp; each electrode is connected to an amplifier which boosts the weak electrical current emanating from the millions of neurons beneath it. The EEG enables the investigator to "listen in" on the millions of neurons beneath the patch of scalp the electrode is pasted to.  To get an idea of what kind of information is provided by the EEG, imagine yourself in the Goodyear Blimp floating 500 feet over the 50 yard line at the Super bowl.  You suspend a microphone 100 feet down from the blimp and record the noise of the crowd.  This microphone isn't sensitive enough to pick up what one person (or one neuron, in the case of our EEG analogy) is saying, but when you listen to the output from the microphone, you can tell when the game starts and ends, when someone has made a touchdown, when a referee has assessed a penalty, when a time-out has been called, and when the half-time show goes on. By listening to the output from the microphone, you get a general idea of what's happening in the stadium.  This is what the EEG does- it gives us a general idea of what is happening with each group of neurons lying beneath each electrode on the scalp.  Each group of neurons numbers in the millions. When EEG machines are connected to sophisticated computers and software, investigators have been able to "hear" conversations between clusters of individual neurons deep in the brain's hypothalamus and others in the cerebral cortex, the former keeping the latter neurons awake, helping to preserve the body's muscle tone (Takahashi, Lin, & Sakai, 2008).

The output of an EEG machine can be processed through computers to represent a real time three-dimensional image of the brain's electrical activity in response to certain stimuli.  Computer, x-ray and radio technology have teamed up to give researchers several powerful non-invasive brain imaging tools give researchers a close-to- real time look at the activity of a living brain.
 

The CT (computed tomography) scan uses an x-ray machine and high speed computers called array processors to produce very detailed images of slices of brain tissue.  The CT scanner is very good at revealing brain damage, but tells us nothing about what parts of the brain are active at the moment.  The PET (positron emission tomography) scanner enables researchers to see what areas of the brain are active at a given moment in time.  The subject receives an injection of temporarily radioactive glucose which the PET scanner tracks as it is sent to active portions of the brain via the bloodstream.  Glucose is the fuel the brain uses to process information.  A PET scanner is essentially a computer driven radiation detector which locates where in the brain the glucose is being used, and provides the researcher with a three-dimensional map of the brain showing the areas of radioactive glucose metabolism.  When coupled with an EEG machine which shows the electrical activity of the surface of the brain in real time, imagery from a PET scanner fills out the complete three-dimensional map of brain activity by showing us the concurrent activity for the entire brain. 

Radio and computer technology combine to give us the MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) scanner, which aligns the spinning atoms in the head by way of a powerful magnetic field, then disrupts them with a burst of radio waves.  When the atoms return to their normal orientation under the influence of the magnetic field, they give off energy which is detected by the MRI scanner and processed into a picture of the brain's soft tissues.  MRI images can show us abnormalities in fluid distribution in the brain, which can be indicative of schizophrenia or other mental disorders (McCarley et al., 1999).  But the MRI gives us only a snapshot, a "still photo" of the brain.  Researchers have figured out how to take MRI pictures less than a second apart, using the iron contained in all blood cells as a reference to blood flow and interpolated brain function. This technique is called "fast MRI" or "functional MRI" and shows what parts of the brain are responding to stimuli by measuring blood flow to active areas.  The PET scanner measures fuel consumption of areas of the brain; the functional MRI measures oxygen consumption of active brain areas.  Both these measures give us reliable estimates of the activity of discrete brain areas in almost real time. Scientists using an MRI machine  have discovered that when a person is lying,  four different regions of the brain react  more strongly than they do when the person is telling the truth.  Perhaps an MRI machine will be fashioned into a future lie-detector: stay tuned, a "No-Lie MRI" franchise may open near you (Pearson, 2006).

Brain Structures

The Brain Stem


Starting at the base of the brain, we find the brainstem, the phylogenetically oldest and most primitive region where the spinal cord enters the skill and enlarges slightly. This area monitors the primary vegetative processes of the body such as heart rate, breathing, blood chemistry and so on. It is here that nerve fibers from one side of the body cross over to the other side of the brain, so that the left side of the brain controls the right side of the body, and vice versa.  We will address the topic of the "split brain" later in this lesson.

Inside the brainstem, we find the reticular activating system (RAS), a network of nerve fibers which radiates from the brainstem to the brain's relay station, the thalamus, which resembles a pair of eggs sitting atop the brainstem.  The RAS is vital for arousal and wakefulness, if the RAS is damaged; the animal is permanently comatose.  If the RAS is electrically stimulated, the sleeping animal awakens immediately.
 

The Thalamus


The thalamus serves as the brain's relay station, routing information from sensory neurons to areas in the brain where the sensory information is processed and interpreted. Information coming from higher centers in the brain is routed through the thalamus to the cerebellum (which is involved in muscular coordination and learning and memory) and to the brainstem.  Neurons vital to visual attention are located in the thalamus.  The thalamus is also vital for consciousness.  In the 1970's, a young woman named Karen Anne Quinlan passed out and stopped breathing after mixing alcohol and tranquilizers at a party.   She never regained consciousness.  Her parents wanted to disconnect her from the machines keeping her alive, but the hospital refused, setting the stage for one of the first highly publicized medical ethics cases involving death with dignity.  When her parents finally obtained a court order to disconnect her from the respirator, she continued to live for another five years in a persistent vegetative state.  Her vital functions kept going, but her awareness was long gone.  What remained were reflexes and the basic housekeeping functions controlled by her brain stem.  The relay station in her thalamus was found on autopsy to be seriously damaged by oxygen deprivation when she stopped breathing after passing out.
 

The Cerebellum


Hanging like an afterthought at the rear of the brainstem, the cerebellum has the appearance of two small, wrinkled hemispheres.  The cerebellum is involved in muscular coordination (squirrels have a large cerebellum) and in certain types of learning and memory. The cerebellum automates and coordinates many of the fine motor behavior sequences we use on a day to day basis, so that we are able to take for granted tying our shoes and climbing stairs.
 

The Limbic System


The limbic system consists of a number of discrete structures located in the middle of the brain- these structures were once referred to as the "midbrain."  The limbic system is instrumental in the expression of  emotions and of  the primary motivations toward   food, water and sex.  The limbic system regulates motives and emotions primarily through a hormonal mechanism, using the pituitary gland as its communication agent.  A pair of almond shaped structures, the amygdala, has been shown to be instrumental in the expression of rage and fear, with different parts of the structure apparently responsible for each.  The amygdala also plays a significant role in social cognition and in the recognition of facial expressions (Nishijo et al., 2003). If the amygdala is removed or lesioned, an animal is very slow to anger.  If the amygdala is stimulated by an electric current, the animal will fly into a rage, or cower in fear, depending on which part of the amygdala is stimulated. In the 1960's, people with tendencies toward violent behavior were (with their consent) given lesions to their amygdalae to see if such psychosurgery could lessen the intensity of their rage (1991).  In a few cases, the lesions did reduce the incidence of patients' rage, but often with undesirable side effects.  And sometimes the rage behavior would return after one or two years, as the patients were fairly young (early 20's) and their brains were able to shift the origins of the rage behavior to other brain structures which were undamaged in the surgery.  Psychosurgery is a treatment for seizure disorders that can't be controlled by medication.
 

The Hippocampus

Located astride the limbic system is a structure shaped like a C-clamp which is responsible for encoding short term memory into long term memory.  Damage to this structure results in patients who are unable to remember anything for more than a few seconds.  Chronic alcoholism, for example, destroys much of the hippocampus resulting in Korsokoff's syndrome, which is characterized by the inability to learn and recall anything new.  Yet people with Korsokoff's syndrome can remember clearly events from their lives which were remembered before their heavy drinking killed off most of the cells in their hippocampus.  On a brighter note, MRI scans have shown the hippocampi of  novice London taxi drivers to grow significantly in size as they learned "the Knowledge-" the huge amount of navigational information about every address in London which is required before they can get a taxi driver's license driving one of London's  iconic "black cabs". Uber threatens to  change all that by getting drivers licensed  as cabs if they have a GPS unit in their vehicle.. Drivers with 40 years experience have some of the biggest hippocampi of all the drivers. The correlation between the increase in size and years of cab driving was most pronounced in the right hippocampus, suggesting that the left hippocampus plays a different role in storing experiences gained in taxi driving.
 

The hypothalamus, which lies just below the thalamus, plays an important role in the regulation of hunger, thirst, body temperature and sexual behavior.  The hypothalamus is the part of the limbic system connected to the body's master gland, the pituitary.  The hypothalamus is the "conductor" of the symphony of the emotional arousal response.  Back in the 1950's, James Olds and Peter Milner found that when they implanted an electrode in the hypothalamus of a rat, the rat would press a switch (up to 7000 times an hour) to deliver an electrical stimulus to its hypothalamus until he dropped from exhaustion.   Rats would even cross an electrical grid to get a jolt off the implanted electrode, a grid which a starving rat wouldn't cross to get food. It turned out that Olds and Milner had found the "pleasure center" in the brain.  Nowadays we call this area (the lateral hypothalamus) the "reward center."  Subsequent investigations  have found similar reward centers in goldfish, dolphins and monkeys.  It turns out that there is both a generalized reward center that triggers the release of the neurotransmitter dopamine, and specific reward centers such as the nucleus accumbens, located in the limbic system and found to be an important component of the brain's "reward circuit" involved in establishing and maintaining addictions.  The late Timothy Leary joked that people could have electrodes implanted in their reward centers as an alternative to drugs.  Some researchers believe that addictions result from a reward deficiency syndrome, a genetic predisposition leading to malfunction of the brain's reward system, leading people to crave substances which are associated with past pleasures.
 

The Cerebral Cortex


The wrinkled surface of the brain is referred to as the cerebral cortex (cortex meaning "bark"  [as in tree] in Latin).  The thin layer of neurons comprising the surface of the brain is wrinkled in order to pack more of it into the skull.  If you were to stretch the surface of the brain onto a flat surface, it would cover about the area of a newspaper page.  The cerebral cortex is replete with neural networks and supported by glial cells- "glue cells" which form a scaffold on which the neurons hang, and serve to feed the neurons and eliminate their wastes.  Some researchers believe that glial cells also participate in information processing, transmission and memory (Todd, Serrano, Lacaille, & Robitaille, 2006) Indeed, some researchers feel that the glial cells are the Rodney Dangerfields of brain cells: they get no respect.  Recent work has shown that glial cells influence the growth of synapses by neurons, and may even influence the formation of memories.  Albert Einstein  had an above average number of glial cells in his brain, together with an absence of a major fissure in the brain found in just about everyone.  The empty space normally taken by the fissure was filled with neurons and glia cells, which probably gave him an advantage in mathematical and conceptual ability, compared with the rest of us mortals.

Each brain is divided into four parts, or lobes.  The frontal lobes are (you guessed it) at the front of the brain behind your forehead; the parietal lobes are at the top to the rear of the cortex; the two temporal lobes are located behind each ear, and the occipital lobes are at the back of your head.  These lobes are divided by fissures, or folds, and serve as geographical "regions" of the brain for study.

Speech and language processing is generally done in at least three distinct areas in the temporal lobes: if you are left-handed, speech processing is done in your right temporal lobe; if you are right-handed, the left temporal lobe handles speech processing.  The occipital lobe processes visual information received from both eyes, which is then sent to other brain areas for more specialized processing of visual form, texture, movement and color.  There are also specialized neurons which are devoted entirely to recognizing and processing faces.
 

Motor function is handled by a one-inch strip of brain tissue running from the bottom to the top of the left and right parietal lobe- neurophysiologists have located the areas in the "motor strip" which control everything from the tips of your toes to the tip of your tongue.  It turns out that the greater the control we have over a particular body part (the lips and hands, for instance), the greater the amount of brain tissue devoted to it.  For instance, the thumb has more brain tissue devoted to controlling it than does the elbow.

Sensory function is handled in a similar way by the brain.  Adjacent each motor strip (the one on the right side of the head, controlling  the left side of the body, and the one on the left side of the head which controls the right side of the body) there is a similarly sized strip of brain tissue devoted to receiving and processing sensory input from each side of the body.  As you may have guessed, there is more sensory tissue devoted to processing information from the hands and lips than there is brain tissue devoted to processing information from the knee and ankle.

The bulk of the cerebral cortex is not committed to sensory, motor, or speech processing activity.  This area comprises about 75% of the cortex and is referred to as the "association area" where connections are made between stored memories and sensory input.  The association areas in the frontal lobes are active in planning sequences of events in the future and in making judgments and intuitions.  Frontal lobe damage can also reduce one's inhibitions, as was the case with Phineas Gage who was "lobotomized" by a flying steel bar in 1848. The story of the brain is not only one of excitation of neurons, but of inhibition of neurons as well.  The frontal lobes are apparently vital in maintaining restraint on impulsive and self-centered impulses.  The frontal lobes are also the first to be affected when people drink alcohol, which alters judgment and social behavior before it affects muscular coordination and alertness.The frontal lobes are also the last brain system to fully develop, and not until about the age of 20: the teenage brain is a work in progress, which partly explains teens' tendency toward impulsive behavior.

The brain divides it functions over a large number of neural networks, which work in concert to produce speech, thinking, and conscious awareness. Much of our brain's activity goes on without our conscious awareness.  The world of which we are aware is much like the tip of an iceberg, representing only about one seventh of the total universe of brain activity.
 
 

The Split Brain


When you look at a human brain from above, it appears to be divided longitudinally  into two halves.  It has long been known that the left side of the brain is involved in speech, reading, writing, mathematical reasoning and understanding.  For years, the right side of the brain was under-rated, and referred to as the "subordinate" side.  In the early 1960's, surgeons severed the large bundle of nerve fibers (the corpus callosum) which connected the two hemispheres of the brain in an attempt to stop epileptic seizures from spreading from one side of the brain to the other.   The surgery was successful, and the patients did not appear to suffer any ill effects from the procedure.  But the corpus callosum, consisting of some 200 million nerve fibers capable to carrying one gigabit of information per second must have an important purpose.  It doesn't exist just to keep the two hemispheres of the brain from sagging.  Nature, you may recall, never does anything just for fun.

Researchers found that if you put an unseen object into the left hand of a split brain patient, he denied it was there. This is because the connection between the patient's left hand and the part of the brain which identifies what that hand is holding has been cut.  The same relationship holds for the left side of a patient's visual field: they can tell you an object is there, but they can't identify it.  However, if you show them a picture in their left visual field of three different objects, they can use their left hands to identify by touch the objects on the screen which match up with the objects they felt with their hands.  None of this processing was done using language, and indeed, the subjects were surprised that they could pick out what they saw without knowing (linguistically) what they saw.  These subjects appeared to be of two minds, with the right hemisphere capable of understanding simple requests while being superior to the left hemisphere at drawing, recognizing faces, perceiving differences, and expressing emotions.
 

The body is bilaterally symmetrical, with pairs of organs which perform basically the same functions.  The human brain, on the other hand, has evolved into a system that has unequally divided up its functions, perhaps to accommodate the fairly recently evolved capability for spoken language.  Even deaf people use their left hemisphere to process sign language.

Music appears to be processed independent of speech in the brain.  After suffering a stroke in 1953, the Russian composer Vissarion Shebalin could no longer understand speech, but he  continued to write music until he died ten years later. On the other hand, the composer Maurice  Ravel lost the ability to speak and write music in 1933, yet he remembered his old compositions and could play scales.  Ravel died four years later of complications from brain surgery.

Most people recognize pictures faster and with greater accuracy when they are flashed to the left visual field (which projects mostly to the right hemisphere; but they recognize words faster when they are flashed to the right visual field, which projects mostly to the left hemisphere.

The past twenty years has seen researchers unravel some of the brain's secrets- we know that the brain processes information in levels, starting with the raw data of sensory experience and analyzing that information in order to reconstruct an image of "reality" as we see it.  But reality is, to a great extent, what we make of it.  There is so much information coming in and so little time to process it.  So the brain "sees what it wants to see," using the mechanisms of sensation and perception, which we will address in the next lesson.
